Lead, Transuranic Materials and Prohibited Objects Recognition by Real Time Radiography
Hanna E. Makaruk
 

Real Time Radiography (RTR) is one of the most important techniques of nondestructive assay used for many years at the Laboratory, and at many other places. It is an active imaging technique, applying an X-ray beam, adjustable in both power and wavelength. The RTR hardware is relatively simple, robust and inexpensive, commercially produced. It works both indoor and outdoor, in any weather and temperature conditions acceptable for its human operators.

We propose to write software, which would greatly extend applicability of RTR systems.

Software requirements:
  • Significant quality improvement of the X-ray images.
  • Easy identification of prohibited objects, even from partial images.
  • Detection of Lead, Uranium, Transuranic Materials.
  • It will be tested for detection of transuranic materials hidden inside lead blocks.
  • Easy to use by RTR operators.
  • Expanded software for professional off-line users.
The RTR system consists of a rotating table, on which drums/cargo pieces are placed; a movable and adjustable cone X-ray beam; a camera, recording image of a rotated drum; a controlling monitor, on which real-time image from the camera is visible; video recording of this image. Collimator crystal limits the diameter of the X-beam. Due to this fact only projection of a small part of the piece of cargo is usually visible at any single picture. Additionally, objects inside a container frequently occlude each other. Identification of an object, when only a part of the object is visible is currently one of the most difficult tasks. Nonparallel projection and low image quality are the other difficulties.

This project concerns analysis of digitized images from RTR. Two versions of the program for image enhancement, objects and materials detection are proposed. Highly automatized version is designed for real-time use by RTR operators. An extended version of the same program would be addressed to professional staff, for work off-line with difficult RTR tapes.

The main tasks performed by the proposed program: image enhancement; concatenation of individual pictures to provide images of big objects; fitting virtual models of prohibited items from a special library to their real-life counterparts by proper rotation and scaling; recognition of X-ray absorbing materials. It is relatively easy to differentiate X-ray pictures of lead and uranium/transuranic materials from all other materials. Proper tuning of software to parameters of a particular X-ray beam would provide possibility to differentiate between lead and all heavier than lead materials. Tests will be performed to establish how clearly this system would identify uranium/transuranic materials hidden inside lead bricks. Nuclear materials, which are heavily lead-shielded do not produce detectable radiological signals. It makes such materials practically unrecognizable for any passive measurement. RTR technique is an active technique based on differences in X-ray absorption. It allows finding such materials in cases that their intrinsic radiation is fully shielded. We propose the test measurements on lead shielded depleted uranium. The choice of such materials would not change the test results; it is driven by ALARA, but is also cost effective and advisable due to security.

Real Time Radiography (RTR) system is used at LANL for characterization of transuranic and/or chemically hazardous waste before their disposal. When RTR is insufficient to exclude presence of a prohibited item inside a drum, such drum has to be manually opened, examined and repacked. Opening a drum is costly and it causes radiation exposure to the workers. In many cases (up to 50% of openings) the operation is performed unnecessarily, due to insufficient data from RTR images. Digital methods of image enhancement and object identification would significantly reduce the percentage of unnecessarily opened drums. It would effectively save LANL opening of thousands of drums during the next few years.

It is a new project, but one of the investigators (HM), has extensive experience with RTR images interpretation at TRU waste characterization project, where for 3 years she was responsible for reconciliation of RTR results with the waste generators data. LANL has highly trained technicians who are the leading experts in performing RTR, and working RTR systems. It would be easy and cost effective to perform software tests on test packages at LANL. The developed software would have numerous potential applications in border security; air cargo security; nuclear waste characterization at LANL as well as other sites, which posses nuclear waste. Proposed test is nondestructive, fast, RTR system is very robust, working in practically any indoor and outdoor conditions, relatively inexpensive, may be performed by moderately trained operators, safe for humans and environment.
