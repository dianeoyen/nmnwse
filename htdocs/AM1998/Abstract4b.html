Affinity Research Groups: Preparing Students to Meet the Challenges of the New Millennium

Drs. Ann Q. Gates, Patricia J. Teller,
Connie Kubo Della-Piana, Andrew Bernat,
Nelly Delgado

Abstract

The Systems and Software Engineering Affinity Lab (SSEAL) at the University of Texas at E1 Paso, funded by the National Science Foundation, provides undergraduate and graduate students the opportunity to engage in research projects and develop their research, technical, communication, and group skills through on-going structured activities. Built upon a cooperative group model, the goals of SSEAL are (1) to increase the retention and participation of traditionally underrepresented groups in the computing areas, and (2) to provide a socialization and infrastructure mechanism for involving students with a wide range of experiences, talents, interests, and skill levels in research and outreach projects. The SSEAL infrastructure takes into account issues that affect all students, but especially those of underrepresented groups, and identifes ways that research groups can effectively address persistence factors, structure skill development, and provide a supportive environment for enabling student success. Specifically, the SSEAL model provides students with opportunities to interact with professors and other students around substantive topics, educates students about the benefits of engaging in research, and facilitates their understanding and appreciation for the research process and its practice. The talk will include techniques professors use to manage large-scale research groups with students at varying levels of expertise and an evaluation of students' experiences in the research group.
