New Mexico Water Quality for the New Millennium
Kelly Bitner

Abstract

In 1972 the architects of the Clean Water Act included a triennial review of water quality standards. They realized that very little was known about the quality of water, uses of water, and how to attain the quality to support the desired use. The triennial review was intended to be a built-in feedback loop wherein knowledge gained from implementing the Clean Water Act could be routinely incorporated in the water quality standards set by each state. The New Mexico Triennial Review is scheduled for September 21, 1998. The standards set by the Water Quality Control Commission based on this triennial review will be the standards New Mexico takes into the new millennium.

Water quality in the new millennium has some tough issues to tackle. The biggest is that 97% of New Mexico water does not attain standards due to non-point sources. This means that only 3% of the degradation of streams is due to point sources that can be controlled. Non-point sources are not regulated and are difficult to control. A second issue is setting standards that make sense for ephemeral streams. Ephemeral streams have no self-sustaining fish populations, so applying standards to protect fish is inappropriate. Wildlife that use the ephemeral streams must be protected, but that numerical standards applied should reflect the ecosystems that exist in association with ephemeral streams.

The triennial review process involves the submittal of proposed language changes to the Water Quality Control Commission. The New Mexico Environment Department is required to submit proposed changes to the standards. Any other person is allowed to propose changes. The Water Quality Control Commission must sift through all of the proposed changes and the testimony that supports or refutes those changes to develop the final language that will be adopted as the New Mexico Standards for Interstate and Intrastate Streams.
