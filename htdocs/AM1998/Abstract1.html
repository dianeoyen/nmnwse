The Odyssey of Bureaucracy: Helping Young Women Navigate Their Way -
Beginning and Implementation of a University-based Mentoring Program
Cameron Langner and Nachelle Pell

Abstract

The primary focus is undergraduate women ages 18-25 with serious interest in the physical sciences, mathematics, engineering and architecture.

The goal is removing the anonymity from the student experience as well as fostering mutually beneficial relationships between faculty/community and students. A challenge of most mentorship programs is/has been recruiting and then retaining mentors. A problem we hope to address and solicit feedback on.

The intention of our program is to increase visibility of women who are successful in scientific careers. By doing so, these professions become accessible. Researchers have suggested that entering students will fare better if they are to "get to know one individual on campus who knows you are there and who cares about your survival" (John Gardner, Your College Experience: Strategies for Success).

Previous programs at UNM show that women are more likely than men to request and use such programs if they are available (see One-On-One Program Results from 1988-89 and 1 989-90).

Mentoring is, by far, one of the most significant ways to bring changes necessary for women to feel welcomed and a part of the scientific fields.
